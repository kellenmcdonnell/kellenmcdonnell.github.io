Graphing Data Unit Plan

Overview:

  • Grade: 8
  • Time: 10 days
  • Class Length: 40 minutes
  • Unit Topic: This unit plan covers univariable data and bivariable data representations.
  • Essential Questions:
    • How do we differentiate between different types of data?
    • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
    • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?
  • Objectives:
    • Students will be able to differentiate between qualitative and quantitative data
    • Students will be able to interpret different kinds of graphs.
    • Students will be able to plot data on a scatter plot.
    • Students will be able to recognize graph patterns and make predictions and draw conclusions.
    • Students will be able to evaluate the effectiveness of different graphical representation.
    • Students will be able to choose appropriate representations for given data sets.
    • Students will be able to use technology to record, graph, and interpret data.
  • Standards Met:
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.A.2 Understand that a set of data collected to answer a statistical question has a distribution which can be described by its center, spread, and overall shape.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.A.3 Recognize that a measure of center for a numerical data set summarizes all of its values with a single number, while a measure of variation describes how its values vary with a single number.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.B.4 Display numerical data in plots on a number line, including dot plots, histograms, and box plots.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.B.5 Summarize numerical data sets in relation to their context, such as by: Reporting the number of observations; Describing the nature of the attribute under investigation, including how it was measured and its units of measurement; Giving quantitative measures of center (median and/or mean) and variability (interquartile range and/or mean absolute deviation), as well as describing any overall pattern and any striking deviations from the overall pattern with reference to the context in which the data were gathered; Relating the choice of measures of center and variability to the shape of the data distribution and the context in which the data were gathered.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.1 Construct and interpret scatter plots for bivariate measurement data to investigate patterns of association between two quantities. Describe patterns such as clustering, outliers, positive or negative association, linear association, and nonlinear association.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.2 Know that straight lines are widely used to model relationships between two quantitative variables. For scatter plots that suggest a linear association, informally fit a straight line, and informally assess the model fit by judging the closeness of the data points to the line.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.3 Use the equation of a linear model to solve problems in the context of bivariate measurement data, interpreting the slope and intercept.

Construct and interpret a two-way table summarizing data on two categorical variables collected from the same subjects. Use relative frequencies calculated for rows or columns to describe possible association between the two variables.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.A.1 Represent data with plots on the real number line (dot plots, histograms, and box plots).
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.B.6.B Represent data on two quantitative variables on a scatter plot, and describe how the variables are related. Informally assess the fit of a function by plotting and analyzing residuals.
    • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.B.6.C Fit a linear function for a scatter plot that suggests a linear association.
    • CCSS.ELA-Literacy.RST.6-8.7 Integrate quantitative or technical information expressed in words in a text with a version of that information expressed visually (e.g., in a flowchart, diagram, model, graph, or table).
    • CCSS.ELA-Literacy.RST.6-8.8 Distinguish among facts, reasoned judgment based on research findings, and speculation in a text.
    • CCSS.ELA-Literacy.RST.9-10.7 Translate quantitative or technical information expressed in words in a text into visual form (e.g., a table or chart) and translate information expressed visually or mathematically (e.g., in an equation) into words.
    • CCSS.ELA-Literacy.WHST.6-8.1 Write arguments focused on discipline-specific content. Introduce claim(s) about a topic or issue, acknowledge and distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and organize the reasons and evidence logically. Support claim(s) with logical reasoning and relevant, accurate data and evidence that demonstrate an understanding of the topic or text, using credible sources. Use words, phrases, and clauses to create cohesion and clarify the relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence. Establish and maintain a formal style. Provide a concluding statement or section that follows from and supports the argument presented.

Introduce a topic clearly, previewing what is to follow; organize ideas, concepts, and information into broader categories as appropriate to achieving purpose; include formatting (e.g., headings), graphics (e.g., charts, tables), and multimedia when useful to aiding comprehension. Day 1 - Introduction to Data

Essential Questions:

  • How do we differentiate between different types of data?
  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to gather basic quantitative and qualitative data
  • Students will be able to plot data using bar graphs

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.A.1 Represent data with plots on the real number line (dot plots, histograms, and box plots).

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board: “What data can we gather from a bag of M&Ms?”

Materials:

  • One bag of M&Ms for each student.

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [6 minutes]
  2. Quantitative vs Qualitative Discussion [10 minutes]
  3. M&M Statistics [24 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [6 min]
    • As students walk in, the instructor will ask students to brainstorm answers to the question on the board.
    • After a few minutes, the instructor will ask students for some of their ideas and write them on the board.
  2. Qualitative vs Quantitative [10 min]
    • The instructor will explain that data can separated into two types: quantitative and qualitative. Quantitative data is data that can be numerically represented while qualitative data cannot be.
    • For each piece of data on the board, the instructor will conduct a vote to see if students think it is qualitative or quantitative. If the class votes for the wrong answer the instructor can say that "math isn't a democracy" and explain why the data is what it is.
  3. M&M Statistics [24 minutes]
    • The instructor will circle a few pieces of data from the board (eg. number of colors, what they are, number of pieces, color distribution, etc.) and tell students to first write down a guess and then find that data for each of their individual bags of M&Ms. After asking for questions, the instructor will have students get their M&Ms.
    • After a couple minutes, the instructor will tell the class that they are going to combine their data on the number of each color in the bag.
    • The instructor will draw a bar graph on the board and add height to the bars as each student gives his/her data.
    • The instructor will ask if the data is what the students expected, and if their data looks the same or different.
    • The instructor will ask the students if they think that all bags of M&Ms follow the same statistical trend as the class data.
    • The instructor will ask students if they think that the large bar graph is more representative than their graphs. Talk about how sample size is important in statistics and that the more data you get, the more representative your results will be.

Assessment:

  1. In the discussion part of M&M Statistics the instructor will give informal feedback if students are correct or not.

Homework:

  1. There is no mandatory homework, but the instructor may offer bonus points on the mid-unit assessment for students who bring in a data set and a bar graph.
Day 2 - Centers and Spreads

Essential Questions:

  • How do we differentiate between different types of data?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to enter data in Excel
  • Students will be able to plot a histogram in Excel
  • Students will be able to calculate mean, median, and standard deviation in excel

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.A.2 Understand that a set of data collected to answer a statistical question has a distribution which can be described by its center, spread, and overall shape.
  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.A.3 Recognize that a measure of center for a numerical data set summarizes all of its values with a single number, while a measure of variation describes how its values vary with a single number.
  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.B.5 Summarize numerical data sets in relation to their context, such as by: Reporting the number of observations; Describing the nature of the attribute under investigation, including how it was measured and its units of measurement; Giving quantitative measures of center (median and/or mean) and variability (interquartile range and/or mean absolute deviation), as well as describing any overall pattern and any striking deviations from the overall pattern with reference to the context in which the data were gathered; Relating the choice of measures of center and variability to the shape of the data distribution and the context in which the data were gathered.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board: “What is the center of a data set? What is the spread?”

Materials:

  • One computer per student with access to Excel
  • One project for the instructor's computer
  • Excel Data Worksheet

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Graphing in Excel [7 minutes]
  3. Measures of Center [7 minutes]
  4. Measure of Variability [8 minutes]
  5. Excel Data Exercise [15 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor will have students give them a piece of data to write down (eg. shoe size) and then ask them to attempt to answer the question on the board independently on a piece of paper.
  2. Graphing in Excel [7 minutes]
    • The instructor will have students get out their laptops and open Excel. The instructor should project a blank Excel spreadsheet.
    • Using the data collected from students, the instructor will enter the data into the spreadsheet and have the students follow along.
    • The instructor will walk students through creating a histogram of the data. The instructor will explain that a histogram represents the frequency distribution (how often certain values occur in a sample) of a data set.
    • The instructor will briefly mention the common "bell curve" and how data in that shape is concentrated at a central point with fewer instances of extreme results.
  3. Measures of Center [7 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that the center of a data set is the value that summarizes the set (as well as one value can). It is the "typical" value, the one a person would expect.
    • The instructor will crowdsource ways to measure the center of a data set, there are several.
    • The instructor will tell the students that the most commonly used measures are the mean and the median. Then, the instructor will show students how to calculate these values in Excel (using the data set from the hook) as they follow along.
  4. Measures of Spread [8 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that the spread of a data set is a value that exhibits how similar the data points are to each other.
    • The instructor will ask if students can think of any ways to measure the spread of a data set.
    • The instructor can lead students to the answer by explaining that when measuring spread, they are looking to summerize the distance between the points. In this way, they can take the average of the distances of each point from the center (the mean). This value is called the standard deviation.
    • The instructor will demonstrate how to calculate standard deviation in Excel (using the data from the hook) as the students follow along.
  5. Excel Data Exercise [15 minutes]
    • The instructor will have students complete the Excel Data Worksheet, allowing them to work with their nearby peers.
    • As students work, the instructor will circulate and help students as necessary.
    • Students should pass in completed worksheets at the end of class or take them home to finish and pass in the next day.

Assessment:

  1. During the independent working time, the instructor can give students informal feedback. The instructor should take note of students who may be struggling and consider ways to ensure they do not fall behind.
  2. After class, the instructor can review the answers from the hook to get a sense for how the students approach unfamiliar topics. These should not be officially graded, only used as a barometer.

Homework:

  1. Any unfinished worksheets should be completed for homework.
  2. The instructor may offer bonus points on the mid unit assessment for students who find a data set and find the mean, median, and standard deviation of it for homework.
Day 3 - Interpretting Single-Variable Data

Essential Questions:

  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to extrapolate data from bar graphs, pie charts, and histograms.

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.B.4 Display numerical data in plots on a number line, including dot plots, histograms, and box plots.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board: "Determine the mean of the following graph:"

Materials:

  • One laptop per student with access to Excel
  • Hook Graph
  • Graph Estimation Worksheet

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Introduction to Single-variable Data [7 minutes]
  3. Graph Estimations and Calculations [30 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor will have students attempt the question on the board independently.
  2. Introduction to Single-Variable Data [7 minutes]
    • The instructor will ask students what they think single-variable data is. They will then explain that single variable data is data that only has one value and that there are several ways to graphically represent single variable sets.
    • The instructor will then quickly review how to read bar graphs, pie charts, and histograms by drawing basic graphs on the board.
  3. Graph Estimations and Calculations [30 minutes]
    • The instructor will mention that graphs can be good for getting a quick sense for a data set. Estimating information from graphs is an important skill for students to practice.
    • The instructor will give students the graph estimation worksheet to complete, allowing them to work with their nearby peers.
    • As students work, the instructor will circulate and help students as necessary.
    • Students should pass in completed worksheets at the end of class or take them home to finish and pass in the next day.

Assessment:

  1. During the independent working time, the instructor can give students informal feedback. The instructor should take note of students who may be struggling and consider ways to ensure they do not fall behind.
  2. After class, the instructor can grade the worksheet and give formal feedback on student performance.

Homework:

  1. Any unfinished worksheets should be completed for homework.
Day 4 - Graph Evaluation

Essential Questions:

  • How do we differentiate between different types of data?
  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to choose an appropriate representation for give single-variable data sets.

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.6.SP.B.4 Display numerical data in plots on a number line, including dot plots, histograms, and box plots.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board before class: "Which graph seems most readable for the given data set?"

Materials:

  • One laptop per student with access to Excel
  • One projector for the instructor's computer
  • Hook Graphs
  • Representation Evaluation Quiz
  • Chalk Talk Graphs

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Bar vs Pie vs Histogram discussion [7 minutes]
  3. Representation Evaluation Quiz [10 minutes]
  4. Data Set Representation Chalk Talk [20 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor will have students attempt to answer the question on the board independently on a piece of paper.
  2. Bar vs Pie vs Histogram discussion [7 minutes]
    • The instructor will have the students create a list of pros and cons of bar graphs, pie charts, and histograms which will be recorded on the board.
    • The instructor will ask the class answer the hook question with specific reasons.
  3. Representation Evaluation Quiz [10 minutes]
    • The instructor will project the Representation Evaluation Quiz.
    • For each question, the instructor will allow the students to have a brief discussion before taking a vote. If the class votes for the wrong answer the instructor can say that "math isn't a democracy" and explain the flaws in their reasoning.
  4. Data set Representation Chalk Talk [20 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that they students will now be having a silent conversation on improving less-than-ideal graphs.
    • The instructor will explain that students will be split into groups and that each group will review a graph for a couple minutes in which they are expected to think of problems with the graph and potential solutions. Students are only aloud to write, not speak.
    • Then the instructor will split the students up into 5-6 groups, sending each group to a corner of the room with a writing space and chalk talk graph. After 5 minutes or so, tell the students to rotate one location clockwise around the room.
    • Each rotation should be slightly shorter than the last, as there will be more comments from other groups on the graphs already.
    • After serveral or all rotations, the instructor will ask the students what they thought of the exercise and what stood out to them.

Assessment:

  1. The instructor can assess how the class responds during the quiz to get a general sense of comprehension.
  2. During the chalk talk, the instructor can circulate and give feedback to students who have just written by writing checks or questions by their comments.

Homework:

  1. While there is no mandatory homework, the instructor can offer bonus points on the mid unit assessment for students who find a single-variable data set that they believe is not represented in the most ideal form and briefly explain their reasoning and suggestions in writing for homework.

Day 6 - Introduction to Bivariate Data

Essential Questions:

  • How do we differentiate between different types of data?
  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to create two-way frequency tables.
  • Students will be able to read and analyze data on a two-way frequency table.

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.4 Understand that patterns of association can also be seen in bivariate categorical data by displaying frequencies and relative frequencies in a two-way table. Construct and interpret a two-way table summarizing data on two categorical variables collected from the same subjects. Use relative frequencies calculated for rows or columns to describe possible association between the two variables.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board before class: "What are some ways we can see if two pieces of data are related?"

Materials:

  • None.

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Two-Way Frequency Table Activity [15 minutes]
  3. Two-Way Frequency See, Think, Wonder [10 minutes]
  4. Project Work time [12 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor should have students give them 4 piece of data (eg. sex, handedness, age, if they are taller than at least 1 parent, etc. ) and then attempt to answer the question on the board independently on a piece of paper.
  2. Two-Way Frequency Table Activity [10 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that they are now moving into bivariate data, data with two elements.
    • The instructor will ask students if they could guess what a two-way frequency table looks like considering that a one sided frequency table is a histogram.
    • The instructor will draw a Two-Way Frequency table on the board and fill it in with two of the data pieces from the hook.
    • The instructor will then ask students what conclusions they can draw from the frequency table.
  3. Two-Way Frequency See, Think, Wonder [15 minutes]
    • The instructor will have students make two more frequency tables using data from the hook and note what they see, think, and wonder about it on a separate sheet of paper.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.
  4. Project Work time [12 minutes]
    • The instructor will give the students time to work on their projects, reminding them that this should be the last day that they are gathering data.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.

Assessment:

  1. During independent and pair work, the instructor can circulate and give informal feedback. The instructor should take note of students who may be struggling and consider ways to ensure they do not fall behind.
  2. After class, the instructor can review students' see, think, wonder papers for understanding.

Homework:

  1. While there is no mandatory homework, the instructor should encourage students to finish their data collection and choose their form of representation.
Day 7 - Correlation

Essential Questions:

  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to create Scatter Plots.
  • Students will be able to identify positive, negative, and nonexistant correlations.

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.1 Construct and interpret scatter plots for bivariate measurement data to investigate patterns of association between two quantities. Describe patterns such as clustering, outliers, positive or negative association, linear association, and nonlinear association.
  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.B.6.B Represent data on two quantitative variables on a scatter plot, and describe how the variables are related. Informally assess the fit of a function by plotting and analyzing residuals.
  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.HSS.ID.B.6.C Fit a linear function for a scatter plot that suggests a linear association.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor will write the hook question on the board before class: "Do you think there is a relationship between any of the data you just submitted?"

Materials:

  • One computer per student with access to Excel
  • One project for the teacher's computer

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Scatter Plots [15 minutes]
  3. Correlation [5 minutes]
  4. Scatter Plot Exercise [12 miutes]
  5. Project time [10 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor will have students give them their height, shoe size, the time they went to bed last night, and the number of hours of sleep they got last night and then attempt to answer the question on the board independently on a piece of paper.
  2. Scatter Plots [15 minutes]
    • On the board, the instructor will show how you can graph two variables on a coordinate plane to make a scatter plot.
    • The instructor will project their computer and show how to make a scatter plot out of data in Excel using one combination of the data collected from the hook.
    • Before plotting the data however, the instructor should ask the class to vote if they predict that there will be a relationship between the two chosen variables.
    • After plotting the data, the instructor should ask how students can determine if there is a relationship or not based on the graph.
  3. Correlation [5 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that a correlation is a relationship between two variables and how it can be negative or positive.
    • The instructor will ask students to predict what a scatter plot would look like for a positve correlation, a negative correlation, and no correlation.
  4. Scatter Plot Exercise [10 minutes]
    • The instructor will have students make the scatter plots of the other pairs of variables and determine if there is a correlation or not between any of them.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.
  5. Project time [10 minutes]
    • The instructor should remind students that they should have collected all of their data and chosen their representations at this point.
    • The instructor should give students the remainder of class to work on their projects.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.

Assessment:

  1. During independent and pair work, the instructor can circulate and give informal feedback. The instructor should take note of students who may be struggling and consider ways to ensure they do not fall behind.
  2. The instructor should take note where students are on their projects and if they are on track to complete them in time.

Homework:

  1. While there is no mandatory homework, the instructor should encourage students to finish their graphs.
Day 8 - Linear Relationships

Essential Questions:

  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to differentiate between correlation and causation.
  • Students will be able to create fit lines on scatter plots iwth linear relationships.

Standards Met:

  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.2 Know that straight lines are widely used to model relationships between two quantitative variables. For scatter plots that suggest a linear association, informally fit a straight line, and informally assess the model fit by judging the closeness of the data points to the line.
  • CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.8.SP.A.3 Use the equation of a linear model to solve problems in the context of bivariate measurement data, interpreting the slope and intercept.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board before class: "Do you believe that importing Lemons from Mexico reduces the number of highway fatalities?"

Materials:

  • One computer per student with access to Excel
  • One project for the teacher's computer
  • Hook Graph

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Best-Fit Lines and Estimation [17 minutes]
  3. Correlation vs Causation [10 minutes]
  4. Linear Relationship Exercises [10 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor should have students attempt to answer the question on the board.
  2. Best-Fit Lines [17 minutes]
    • Using a scatter plot from Day 7, the instructor should ask students to predict where 'new' data point would fall (eg. a person is 80 inches tall, what do you expect their shoe size to be). It is important to have them explain their thinking.
    • The instructor will explain the concept of 'fitting' a line to a scatter plot, the concept of a linear relationship. Then, they will show the students how to create a best-fit line on a scatter plot in Excel.
    • The instructor will ask students if they can now make an educated guess of where the 'new' data point would fall (using slope and intercept).
    • The instructor will have students estimate some additional points and circulate, assisting as necessary.
  3. Correlation vs Causation [10 minutes]
    • The instructor will ask the students if correlation implies causation. Does growing taller cause one to have a larger shoe size? Does importing lemons cause highway fatalities to decrease?
    • The instructor should emphasize the importance of being skeptical and thinking beyond the presented data. Some data can be very misleading when presented certain ways.
  4. Linear Relationships Exercises [10 minutes]
    • The instructor will have students create a best fit line for the other linear relationship from the day before and decide if they think that there is causation or not.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.

Assessment:

  1. During independent and pair work, the instructor can circulate and give informal feedback. The instructor should take note of students who may be struggling and consider ways to ensure they do not fall behind.

Homework:

  1. While there is no mandatory homework, the instructor should encourage students to start working on their writeups.
Day 9 - Poorly Designed Graphs

Essential Questions:

  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to identify misleading graphs.
  • Students will be able to suggest appropriate alternatives to poorly designed graphs.

Standards Met:

  • None.

Prep Work:

  • Before class, the instructor should write the hook question on the board before class: "Why is this an ineffective graph?"

Materials:

  • Hook Graph
  • Chalk Talk Graphs

Procedure Overview:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
  2. Bad Graphs Chalk Talk [22 minutes]
  3. Project time [15 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Hook [3 minutes]
    • As students walk in, the instructor should have students attempt to answer the question on the board.
  2. Bad Graphs Chalk Talk [22 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that they students will now be having a silent conversation on improving malicious or useless graphs.
    • The instructor will explain that students will be split into groups and that each group will review a graph for a couple minutes in which they are expected to think of problems with the graph and potential solutions. Students are only aloud to write, not speak.
    • Then the instructor will split the students up into 5-6 groups, sending each group to a corner of the room with a writing space and chalk talk graph. After 5 minutes or so, tell the students to rotate one location clockwise around the room.
    • Each rotation should be slightly shorter than the last, as there will be more comments from other groups on the graphs already.
    • After serveral or all rotations, the instructor will ask the students what they thought of the exercise and what stood out to them.
  3. :Project time [15 minutes]
    • The instructor should give students the remainder of class to work on their projects. Students should be encouraged to finish their visuals.
    • While students are working, the instructor should circulate and offer advice as necessary.

Assessment:

  1. During the chalk talk, the instructor can circulate and give feedback to students who have just written by writing checks or questions by their comments.

Homework:

  1. The project is due in its entirety tomorrow.

  • How can we determine the best methods to represent data sets?
  • How does technology help to draw conclusions from data?

Objectives:

  • Students will be able to give an informal presentation on their data set.

Prep Work:

  • None.

Materials:

  • None.

Procedure Overview:

  1. Presentation Setup [5 minutes]
  2. Presentations [25 minutes]
  3. See, Think, Wonder Response [10 minutes]

Procedure:

  1. Presentation Setup [5 minutes]
    • The instructor should give students about five minutes to organize their display somewhere on the edge of the room.
  2. Presentations [25 minutes]
    • The instructor will explain that presentations will be done in two rounds such that one of each pair can go observe the other students' work and the other can answer questions and give a brief presentation.
    • The instructor will tell each student to find the project that stands out to them the most (for any reason) and to write a See, Think, Wonder about it afterwards.
    • The instructor will circulate to each project and have the student give them a brief presentation about their work and results. Make sure to do this each round to hear from both students for each project.
  3. See, Think, Wonder Response [10 minutes]
    • Student write about what they see, think, and wonder about whichever project was standout to them. The instructor should collect these responses before students leave.

Assessment:

  1. The instructor should take notes about the clarity and effectiveness of each students' presentation.
  2. The instructor will grade and give formal feedback on the project to each pair as soon as possible.

Homework:

  1. None.
